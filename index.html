Interpretive Work

Master’s Thesis - UK Graduates Navigating Algorithmic Hiring

Interpretive

MSc Research · LSE Media & Communications (Data & Society)

A qualitative study of how recent graduates in the UK navigate algorithmic hiring platforms such as LinkedIn and applicant tracking systems (ATS). Using reflexive thematic analysis of in-depth interviews, I examine how they understand these systems, try to “win” them, and negotiate the privacy and emotional costs involved. Explore the themes below.

Designing for Algorithmic Legibility

How graduates co-author with AI tools to “speak the system’s language.”

Performing Employability

Branding, keywording, and content hacks to appear hireable to both humans and algorithms.

Networked Advantage

How elite university brands and forced networking act as another optimisation layer.

Data Boundaries & Hidden Labour

The emotional work and ethical trade-offs of constantly feeding platforms with data.

How do students use GenAI for Learning?

Interpretive

Research poster · LSE Changemakers 2024–25

Worked as a Research Assistant under the LSE Changemakers scheme, investigating students’ use of generative AI in learning. Designed and conducted surveys and focus groups to explore how students used GenAI for tasks such as summarizing, drafting, and explaining concepts, and how concerns about privacy and university monitoring influenced their behaviour. Conducted mixed-methods analysis, combining qualitative coding with basic statistical analysis, to identify patterns in students’ trust, perceived risks, and “folk theories” of GenAI tools.
